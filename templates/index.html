Global High Scores

01

Domain Spoofing Tactics

Phishing attackers frequently spoof domains to make emails appear as though they originate from trusted entities.

Common Trick: Character replacements (Look-alike domains), e.g., using a capital I or number 1 instead of a lowercase l (such as paypaI.com vs paypal.com).
Security Rule:

Never click links inside emails displaying warnings about urgent billing revisions without navigating to the website manually through your browser URL bar.

02

Link Hijacking & Mismatches

The label text of a link does not have to match the actual destination address. Attackers write authentic URLs as text labels but route the click elsewhere.

Common Trick: Displaying https://bankofamerica.com while pointing the hyperlink href to http://boa-login-credentials-verify.xyz.
Security Rule:

Hover your cursor over the link before clicking. Look at the status bar at the bottom of your window to confirm the domain matches the brand exactly.

03

Payload Attachments

Opening attachments from unknown sources is the primary route for malware and ransomware payloads.

Common Trick: Double file extensions like invoice.pdf.exe, or macro-enabled documents like invoice.xlsm which download malware.
Security Rule:

Never open files ending in double extensions, and avoid enabling macros for attachments. Keep your system antivirus enabled and updated.

04

SPF / DKIM Checks

Sender Policy Framework (SPF) and DomainKeys Identified Mail (DKIM) are authentication tools that confirm an email actually came from the domain owner.

Common Trick: Bypassing validation with free email hosting services to mimic branding displays (e.g. sending from apple-support@gmail.com).
Security Rule:

Verify that official correspondence from institutional brands (Netflix, Apple, Chase, etc.) matches their domain handles directly (not using generic free accounts).
